%This study explores public preferences regarding tax enforcement and tax administration, focusing on attitudes towards the expansion of third-party reporting and the role of the tax authority in providing tax software and prepopulated tax returns. We conduct a survey experiment on a representative sample of the U.S.\ population. We vary the information that participants obtain to make causal inference on the role that information on tax evasion, inequality, and tax filing costs plays in views on comprehensive third-party reporting. Our results show that without additional information, about half of the respondents are in favor of comprehensive third-party reporting. This number increases up to almost three quarters for participants in our full information treatmet. Free tax software and the pre-population of tax returns by the tax authority are supported by a majority of respondents who have not received additional information and by a vast majority of respondents in our information treatments.

\begin{center}
    \textbf{Extended Abstract}
\end{center}
Despite substantial achievements in tax enforcement in recent decades, %\footnote{While the rise in third-party reported income has expanded modern economies’ tax bases \parencite{jensen2022employment} and reduced domestic tax evasion \parencite{kleven2011unwilling, pomeranz2015no, slemrod2017does}, recent advances in international cooperation on the exchange of tax-relevant information—most notably Automatic Exchange of Information (AEoI) under the OECD’s Common Reporting Standard (CRS) and the US Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA)—have reduced offshore tax evasion \parencite{boas2024taxing}.}
many tax administrations still lack access to significant amounts of tax-relevant data that could help reduce tax evasion. In the context of offshore tax evasion, there is evidence of a shift of offshore wealth towards asset classes that are not covered by international reporting standards \parencite{alstadsaeter2023global,bomare2025avoiding}. In the domestic context many tax administrations lack access to information on sole proprietorships and pass-through businesses.\footnote{While income from sole proprietorships and pass-through businesses (e.g. partnerships and S corporations) accounts for the largest share of the US tax gap, the IRS also lacks information on other income sources, including rents and royalties, income from estates and trusts, gains from the sale of business property, farming income, and other income such as alimony and gambling winnings (GAO, \citeyear{mctigue2019tax})}

As detailed transaction-level reporting can impose a substantial administrative burden on small businesses and consumers, tax administrations often focus on the reporting of aggregate amounts via platforms or banks. Such reporting provides a signal as to where limited enforcement resources should be directed. The Biden administration proposed in 2021 to introduce aggregate bank flow reporting, whereby banks would be required to report inflows and outflows for all accounts above a certain (low) threshold. Despite the scope of this information-sharing intervention being intentionally kept extremely limited, the proposal triggered a substantial data-privacy backlash, which drastically reduced its political support.\footnote{The administrative burden would have been low, as banks, rather than taxpayers, would only need to report two additional aggregate measures—the inflow and outflow for each bank account—without sharing information on individual transactions.}

In contrast to the opposing data privacy concerns that dominate much of the policy debate, there are two key rationales for supporting policies that introduce more comprehensive information exchange. First, such policies have substantial potential to benefit society by increasing tax revenue and improving fairness in the tax system. In the United States, \$696 billion (15\%) of tax liabilities are not paid on time, and \$606 billion (13\%) are never collected \parencite{IRS_TaxGap_2022_Pub5869}.

Second, comprehensive third-party reporting does not only help to enforce tax laws, but it can also improve the service that the tax authority can provide to citizens. In the U.S., taxpayers face high tax filing costs and are urged to purchase commercial tax software to file tax returns. On average, an individual taxpayer needs $13$ hours and about \$$250$ to file their taxes \parencite[][]{IRS2022}—when aggregated over all individual taxpayers in the U.S., this reflects a total cost of approximately \$$100$ billion per year. % Rough calculations: 160 million tax payers * (USD250 + 13 * USD25) = USD 92 billion OR 165 * (250 + 13 * 30) = 105600
Some governments ease the burden of tax filing by offering free tax software. The biggest reductions in filing costs can, however, be achieved by pre-populating tax returns, which is only possible if the tax authority has access to a substantial amount of third-party reported data.  

This paper aims to measure people's understanding, considerations and preferences for information sharing along all these margins. We examine attitudes toward introducing more comprehensive third-party reporting in a large scale survey on a representative sample of the U.S.\ population. We measure support for a policy constructed such that it aligns with the policy debate in the U.S.\ and has potential to reduce tax evasion and filing costs.\footnote{The policy consists of three components: (1) introducing aggregate bank flow reporting (aimed at reducing tax evasion), (2) transmitting previously unreported information on taxes paid at the local level to the IRS (important for prepopulating itemized tax returns), and (3) ensuring that reported data are received more quickly (allowing timely prepopulation of tax returns).} 

We measure how support changes when certain conditions are met by the policy, such as when it focuses primarily on high-income individuals, when the data can only be used for tax enforcement and administration purposes, or when the data is also used to offer prepopulated tax returns. In addition to attitudes to and support for intra-country information sharing, we measure people's attitudes towards, and support for, the international exchange of information.

We furthermore measure attitudes toward and support for services that the tax authority could provide, namely the free provision of tax software and the offering of pre-populated tax returns. We also include additional questions to assess, along several dimensions, whether respondents prefer the government or private companies as providers of such services.

We leverage advances in survey methodology that allow us to identify the considerations and beliefs that drive support for these policies. Prior to eliciting policy views, we ask questions related to the factors and perceptions underlying respondents’ considerations and attitudes. In particular, we focus on respondents’ beliefs and considerations regarding three main factors underlying policy support: (1) tax evasion, (2) tax filing costs, and (3) data privacy concerns.

To elicit beliefs about the policies, we start with questions along a revenue channel, namely, whether the policy generates revenue or is costly to implement, and along an inequality and fairness channel, namely, whether the policy increases fairness and reduces inequality.\footnote{Regarding beliefs about tax evasion, we measure considerations of fairness and inequality in order to address the facts that tax evasion is unevenly distributed \parencite{guyton2021tax,boas2024taxing} and that honest taxpayers are subject to the consequences of tax evasion, because the government must either raise taxes or reduce public services in order to address the resulting tax gap. Regarding beliefs about government-provided tax software, we measure whether individuals perceive the current system—in which private tax preparation software providers charge taxpayers for access to their products to generate profits—as fair.} We then ask respondents to what extent they believe the policy reduces filing costs and to what extent they are concerned about data privacy. Finally, to better understand respondents’ data privacy concerns, we analyze how concerns about third-party reporting vary depending on which types of data would be included.

Additionally, we include questions to measure respondents' demographic background and political leanings, questions to measure their knowledge about underlying factors such as the extent and distribution of tax evasion, questions to measure their exposure to tax enforcement and tax administration policy, and finally, questions to measure their views about the government. These additional questions serve (1) to understand how beliefs and support are distributed among groups and (2) to control for determinants that have shown to be predictive for policy support. 

To establish causal links between considerations on and support for the policies, and to assess the role of misperceptions, we experimentally provide participants with information. Participants are randomized to five treatments: one control group receiving no information and four treatment groups receiving information via short informational videos. The videos provide information on the three main factors underlying policy support.

The first two treatments focus on tax evasion and the revenue effects of information exchange. Treatment 1, \textit{Evasion}, shows information about the overall extent of tax evasion. Treatment 2, \textit{Evasion \& Inequality}, additionally provides information about the distribution of tax evasion. The remaining two treatments focus on tax filing costs. They both provide information about tax software and prepopulated tax returns. Treatment 3, \textit{Filing}, explains how government provided tax software reduces tax filing costs and evaluates the importance of third-party reporting to prepopulate tax returns. Treatment 4, \textit{Filing \& Lobbying}, additionally highlights the presence of an active tax-filing lobby that seeks to preserve the current system.

Differences in elicited considerations and policy views across treatments allow us (i) to examine how individuals’ considerations change when presented with specific information, (ii) to make causal inferences about which underlying considerations drive policy views, and (iii) to explore how different considerations translate into policy views when weighed against one another.

%\begin{center}
\textbf{Related Literature.}
%\end{center} 
Our paper relates to several strands of literature. First, this study contributes to the growing body of research that measures support for economic policies using survey experiments. A part of this literature analyzes preferences over taxation and taxation-related policies. \textcite{stantcheva2021understanding} explores beliefs, reasoning and views on income taxation and estate taxation. \textcite{cruces2013biased} and \textcite{kuziemko2015elastic} analyze inequality perceptions and support of redistributive policies, \textcite{alesina2018intergenerational} and \textcite{alesina2023immigration} analyze the effects of perceptions of intergenerational mobility and immigration on preferences for redistribution. Attitudes toward tax progressivity are examined in \textcite{tarroux2019value}. \textcite{liscow2022psychology} elicit preferences over the realization rule in capital gains taxation. \textcite{fisman2020americans} explore views toward a wealth tax in the U.S. Beyond taxation, survey experiments have, for instance, been used to analyze support for legalizing payments to kidney donors \parencite{elias2019paying} or views on climate policies \parencite{dechezlepretre2025fighting}. Much of the recent research in this field builds on advances in survey methodology, which facilitate uncovering perceptions, attitudes, and reasoning underlying policy views (see \textcite{haaland2023designing} for a discussion on designing information provision experiments and \textcite{stantcheva2023run} for a guide to run survey experiments). 

Second, this project is related to the literature on tax enforcement and tax evasion, especially to the literature on the relevance of information reporting. \textcite{slemrod2007cheating} documents that misreported income in the U.S.\ varies considerably by income type. While income from wages, salaries, interest and dividends (all of which are subject to third-party reporting) is rarely misreported, income from self-employment (typically self-reported) has estimated tax gaps above $50$\%. Similarly, \textcite{kleven2011unwilling} discover near-complete compliance and no deterrence effects for third-party reported income sources in Denmark. Relatedly, \textcite{pomeranz2015no} looks at VAT paper trails in Chile and finds that the effects of randomized deterrence messages are much lower in areas covered by VAT paper trails, suggesting that evasion is much lower in these areas. Other prior work investigates effects of information mechanisms such as consumer receipt collection \parencite{naritomi2019consumers}, the role of digital payment reporting forms like 1099-K \parencite{slemrod2017does}, and discusses the theoretical foundations for why third-party reporting improves compliance \parencite{kleven2016can}. Finally, \textcite{jensen2022employment} documents how modern tax systems arise over time and finds evidence that a rise in third-party reported income drives an expansion of the income tax base. Recent research also shows that the international automatic exchange of information (AEoI) reduces offshore tax evasion \parencite{boas2024taxing,baselgia2023compliance}.\footnote{A key finding of the analysis of offshore tax evasion is that it is much more prevalent among the highest income earners. While \textcite{johns2010distribution} reveal that the net misreporting percentage generally increases with income group but peaks at the $99$th percentile, \textcite{alstadsaeter2019tax} analyze leaked data and conclude that offshore tax evasion is highly concentrated among the super-rich. They estimate that the top $0.01$\% of households evade approximately $25$\% of their taxes. By contrast, tax evasion detected in stratified random tax audits is less than $5$ percent throughout the distribution. These results are consistent with those of \textcite{boas2024taxing}, who analyze offshore tax evasion in Denmark, and \textcite{guyton2021tax}, who study tax evasion at the top of the income distribution in the U.S. They show that previous estimates had failed to capture sophisticated evasion via offshore accounts and pass-through businesses, therefore underestimating top-$1$\% tax evasion.}

Third, this project also relates to the literature on tax compliance costs and the government's role in providing services to reduce them. In an early study on the tax compliance costs of individual taxpayers in the U.S., \textcite{blumenthal1992compliance} find that in 1989 taxpayers needed on average about $27$ hours and \$$66$ (1989 dollars) to file their taxes. In more recent work to understand how costly individuals perceive filing tax returns, \textcite{benzarti2020taxing} observes how taxpayers choose between itemizing deductions and claiming the standard deduction, finding that taxpayers forego large tax savings to avoid compliance costs. \textcite{hauck2024optional} shows that non-filing of tax declarations is particularly common at the lower end of the income distribution in Germany, which reduces the effectiveness of redistributive features of the tax system. \textcite{blesse2019people} present survey evidence eliciting the preferences of German taxpayers regarding the trade-off between simplifying the German tax code and reducing its progressivity. They discover that, while most taxpayers support simplifying the tax system, this support is partly driven by misconceptions about the trade-offs involved in tax complexity. \textcite{benzarti2024rising} ask U.S.\ respondents about the complexity of the tax code. They find that taxpayers perceive an increase in tax complexity and filing costs, that the vast majority of respondents consider tax filing to be tedious, and that almost all respondents would prefer a simpler tax system. Respondents would be willing to pay on average \$$130$ for a simpler tax system (despite the fact that the authors tell respondents that the simpler tax system might come with a higher tax liability due to fewer deduction possibilities). According to \textcite{benzarti2024rising}, the majority of respondents is also supportive of pre-populated tax returns.
